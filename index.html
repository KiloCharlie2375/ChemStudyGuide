Matter & Measurement

  • Matter: Anything that has mass and occupies space. All physical "stuff" in the universe.
  • Types: Substance (element/compound) vs. mixture (homogeneous/heterogeneous).
  • Physical properties: Observed without changing substance identity (color, melting point, density, etc.).
  • Chemical properties: Observed via chemical change (flammability, acidity).
  • States of Matter: Solid, liquid, gas, plasma.

Pure Substance

  • Element: Only one type of atom (O₂, Fe, H₂, etc.)
  • Compound: Two or more elements, chemically combined (H₂O, NaCl, CO₂)

Mixtures

  • Homogeneous (Solution): Uniform throughout (salt water, air)
  • Heterogeneous: Non-uniform, visible phases (sand in water, salad)

When separating mixtures, use physical methods (filtration, distillation, chromatography, evaporation, decanting).

Only compounds can be separated by chemical means (electrolysis, chemical reactions).

  • Physical Change: No new substance (cutting, melting, dissolving, phase changes).
  • Chemical Change: New substance formed (burning, rusting, reacting acids/bases).
Key Signs of Chemical Change:
  • Color change
  • Gas formation (bubbles)
  • Precipitate formation (solid from solution)
  • Heat/light produced or absorbed
Example: Is boiling water a chemical or physical change?
Physical change (only state changes, H₂O molecules stay the same).
Example: Iron rusting?
Chemical change (Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃, new substance formed).
  • Base SI Units:
    • Length: meter (m)
    • Mass: kilogram (kg)
    • Time: second (s)
    • Amount: mole (mol)
    • Temperature: kelvin (K)
  • Prefixes: kilo- (k, 10³), centi- (c, 10⁻²), milli- (m, 10⁻³), micro- (μ, 10⁻⁶), nano- (n, 10⁻⁹)
Example: Convert 500 mg to grams.
500 mg × (1 g / 1000 mg) = 0.5 g
  • Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true value.
  • Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other.
  • Error: Difference between measured and true value.
  • Percent Error: |measured - true| / true × 100
Example: Calculate percent error for a measured mass of 49g (true = 50g)
|49-50|/50 × 100 = 2%

Atoms & The Periodic Table

  • Atom: Smallest unit of matter; consists of protons (+), neutrons (0), electrons (–).
  • Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons, defines the element.
  • Mass Number (A): Protons + neutrons.
  • Isotope: Same element (same Z), different neutrons (different A).
  • Periodic Table: Arranged by atomic number, groups/families (columns), periods (rows).
ParticleSymbolChargeMass (amu)Location
Protonp⁺+11Nucleus
Neutronn⁰01Nucleus
Electrone⁻–1~0Electron cloud
  • Dalton’s Atomic Theory: Atoms are indivisible (now we know they are made of subatomic particles), combine in fixed ratios.
  • Thomson: Discovered electrons (cathode ray tube), “plum pudding” model.
  • Rutherford: Gold foil experiment; atom is mostly empty space, small dense positive nucleus.
  • Bohr: Electrons orbit at set distances (“energy levels”); works well for hydrogen.
  • Quantum Mechanical Model: Electrons in orbitals (probability clouds), not fixed paths.

[Dalton]      [Thomson]        [Rutherford]    [Bohr]             [Modern]
  ⚫             ⚫++--           ⚫ (tiny)         ⚫⎯⎯⦾           ⚫⎯⎯{cloud}
                (e– “in soup”)  (nucleus)    (e– in shells)   (e– in orbitals)
  • Groups (columns): Similar chemical properties, same valence e–
  • Periods (rows): Energy levels (principal quantum number, n)
  • Main families:
    • 1A: Alkali Metals (very reactive, +1)
    • 2A: Alkaline Earth Metals (+2)
    • 7A: Halogens (–1)
    • 8A: Noble Gases (inert, full outer shell)
  • Transition Metals: Variable charges, form colored compounds
  • Metals, Nonmetals, Metalloids: See stair-step on PT for metalloids
Tip: How to find element properties from its location
Group number → valence electrons (for A groups); period number → number of shells.
  • Atomic Radius: Increases ↓ group (more shells); decreases → period (stronger nucleus pulls e– in)
  • Ionization Energy: Decreases ↓ group (e– farther from nucleus, easier to remove); increases → period (more tightly held)
  • Electronegativity: Increases →, decreases ↓ (Fluorine = highest, Francium = lowest)
  • Electron Affinity: More negative →, less negative ↓ (except noble gases)
  • Ionic size: Cations smaller, anions larger than parent atoms
Mnemonic: "F is the greediest!"
Fluorine has the highest electronegativity and electron affinity.
Example: Which atom is larger, Na or K?
K is larger (more energy levels).
  • Isotopes: Same Z, different N (neutrons); identical chemical properties, different mass
  • Average Atomic Mass: Weighted average based on abundance
  • Formula: (mass₁ × %₁ + mass₂ × %₂ + ...) / 100
Practice: Calculate atomic mass for 75% Cl-35, 25% Cl-37
(0.75×35) + (0.25×37) = 26.25 + 9.25 = 35.5 amu
  • Order of fill: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, ...
  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons occupy lowest energy orbitals first
  • Pauli Exclusion: Max 2 e– per orbital (opposite spins)
  • Hund’s Rule: Fill orbitals singly before pairing
  • Noble Gas Notation: Use nearest noble gas in [brackets] as shortcut
Example: Electron configuration of Sulfur (S, Z=16)
1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴ or [Ne] 3s² 3p⁴
  • Misconception: Atomic mass is always a whole number.
    Correction: Atomic mass is often a decimal because it's a weighted average of isotopes.
  • Tip: Memorize 1-2 properties for each major group (alkali, alkaline earth, halogens, noble gases).
  • Misconception: Electrons “orbit” like planets.
    Correction: Electrons are found in orbitals, not fixed paths.

Bonding & Structure

  • Chemical Bond: The attractive force holding atoms or ions together in compounds.
  • Main Types: Ionic (transfer), Covalent (sharing), Metallic (“sea of electrons”).
  • Lewis Dot Structures: Diagrams showing valence electrons and bonding.
  • VSEPR Theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — predicts molecular shapes.
Bond TypeHow FormedPropertiesExample
Ionic Transfer of electrons (metal + nonmetal) High mp/bp, conducts when molten/aq, crystalline NaCl, MgO
Covalent Sharing of electrons (nonmetal + nonmetal) Low mp/bp, not conductive, discrete molecules H2O, CO2
Metallic “Sea of electrons” around positive ions (metal + metal) Conducts, malleable, ductile Fe, Cu, Al
  • Ions: Atoms/molecules with a net charge (cation = +, anion = –).
  • Ionic Compounds: Usually crystalline solids, formula unit shows lowest ratio.
  • Properties:
    • High melting/boiling points
    • Conduct electricity when molten or in solution
    • Brittle, not malleable
  • Electrostatic Forces: Strong attractions between oppositely charged ions.
Example: Why is NaCl solid at room temperature?
Strong ionic bonds require lots of energy to break.

  • Molecular Compounds: Formed from nonmetals sharing electrons.
  • Bond Types:
    • Single: 1 pair shared (H–H)
    • Double: 2 pairs shared (O=O)
    • Triple: 3 pairs shared (N≡N)
  • Lone Pairs: Pairs of electrons not involved in bonding, affect shape (VSEPR).
  • Properties:
    • Low melting/boiling points
    • Poor conductors
    • May exist as gas, liquid, or solid

Q: Why does CO2 have a linear shape?
No lone pairs on central atom, so O=C=O is linear (VSEPR).
  • Metallic Bonds: Metal cations surrounded by a “sea” of delocalized electrons.
  • Properties:
    • Conduct electricity & heat
    • Malleable (bendable), ductile (drawn into wire)
    • Shiny (luster)
  • Alloys: Mixtures of metals (brass = Cu + Zn, steel = Fe + C)
Q: Why are metals good conductors?
Free electrons can move and carry current/energy.
  • Electronegativity: Ability of atom to attract e– in a bond (F = highest, Fr = lowest)
  • Bond Polarity:
    • Nonpolar covalent: ∆EN 0–0.2
    • Polar covalent: ∆EN 0.3–1.7
    • Ionic: ∆EN > 1.7
  • Molecular Polarity: Determined by shape and bond polarity (symmetry = nonpolar, asymmetry = polar)
  • Intermolecular Forces:
    • Dispersion (all molecules)
    • Dipole-dipole (polar molecules)
    • Hydrogen bonding (H with N, O, F)

Gases & Gas Laws

  • Gas: A state of matter with indefinite shape and volume, compressible, fills container.
  • Kinetic Molecular Theory: Gases made of particles in constant, random motion; mostly empty space; elastic collisions.
  • Gas Laws: Relate pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and amount (n, moles) of a gas.
  • STP: Standard Temperature & Pressure (0°C, 273.15 K, 1 atm, 22.4 L/mol).
PropertyDefinitionCommon Units
Pressure (P) Force per unit area atm, kPa, mmHg, torr, psi
Volume (V) Space occupied by gas L, mL, cm³
Temperature (T) Average kinetic energy of particles K (Kelvin, always!), °C (for conversion)
Amount (n) Quantity of gas moles (mol)

Conversions: 1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg = 14.7 psi

  • Gases consist of small particles separated by lots of empty space.
  • Collisions are perfectly elastic (no energy loss).
  • Particles are in constant, straight-line motion.
  • Average kinetic energy ∝ Kelvin temperature (KE ∝ T).
  • Explains compressibility, expansion, low density, and diffusion.
Extension: Real vs Ideal Gases
  • Ideal gas: Obeys KMT at all T & P (doesn't exist, but a good model).
  • Real gas: Deviates at low T/high P (particles have volume, IMFs matter).
  • Boyle’s Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (T, n constant) — P↑ V↓ (inverse)
  • Charles’ Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (P, n constant) — T↑ V↑ (direct, T in K!)
  • Gay-Lussac’s Law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (V, n constant) — T↑ P↑
  • Combined Gas Law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ (n constant)
  • Avogadro’s Law: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂ (P, T constant) — more moles, more volume
  • Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT (R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K or 8.31 kPa·L/mol·K)
  • Dalton’s Law: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + ... (sum of partial pressures)
  • Graham’s Law: Rate₁/Rate₂ = √(M₂/M₁) (diffusion/effusion rates)
Mnemonic: “Can Good People Always Party Down?”
Charles, Gay-Lussac, Boyle, Avogadro, Dalton (Gas Laws)
  1. Boyle’s Law Example: A 2.0 L gas at 1.0 atm is compressed to 0.5 L. What is the new pressure?
    Solution
    P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ ⇒ (1.0 atm)(2.0 L) = P₂(0.5 L) ⇒ P₂ = 4.0 atm
  2. Charles’ Law Example: If 1.5 L of gas at 300 K is heated to 450 K at constant pressure, what’s the new volume?
    Solution
    V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ ⇒ 1.5/300 = V₂/450 ⇒ V₂ = 2.25 L
  3. Ideal Gas Law Example: What volume does 3.0 mol O2 occupy at 2 atm, 273 K?
    Solution
    PV = nRT ⇒ V = nRT/P ⇒ V = (3.0)(0.0821)(273)/2 = 33.6 L
  4. Dalton’s Law Example: A mixture has O2 at 0.2 atm, N2 at 0.7 atm, and CO2 at 0.1 atm. What’s the total pressure?
    Solution
    P_total = 0.2 + 0.7 + 0.1 = 1.0 atm
  5. Graham’s Law Example: Which diffuses faster, H2 or O2?
    Solution
    Rate ratio = √(M_O2/M_H2) = √(32/2) = 4. H2 diffuses 4x faster!
  • Always use Kelvin for temperature in gas calculations.
  • Convert volumes to L, pressures to atm or kPa before plugging into equations.
  • Measure gas volume over water? Correct for water vapor pressure (P_gas = P_total - P_water).
Practice: Water vapor correction
If collected gas at 730 mmHg, and water vapor pressure is 30 mmHg, actual gas pressure = 700 mmHg.
  • van der Waals Equation: Corrects for real gas behavior ((P + a(n/V)²)(V-nb) = nRT)
  • High P/low T: Real gases deviate from ideal (IMFs, particle volume).
  • Derivation of root mean square speed: u_rms = √(3RT/M) (M = molar mass in kg/mol)
AP Example: What causes deviation from ideal gas behavior?
IMFs (attraction between molecules) and non-negligible particle volume.
Challenge: Calculate the density of CO2 at STP
D = PM/RT. P=1 atm, M=44 g/mol, R=0.0821, T=273 K ⇒ D = (1*44)/(0.0821*273) ≈ 1.96 g/L
  • Misconception: Temperature in gas laws can be Celsius.
    Correction: Always use Kelvin (K = °C + 273).
  • Tip: For gases at the same T and P, equal volumes mean equal moles (Avogadro’s Law).
  • Misconception: Gases are ideal at all conditions.
    Correction: Only at high T, low P!

    Hint

Solutions & Molarity

  • Solutions: homogeneous mixtures, solute dissolved in solvent
  • Molarity (M): mol/L
  • Molality (m): mol/kg solvent
  • Dilutions: M₁V₁=M₂V₂
  • Percent by mass: (g solute/g solution) × 100
  • Colligative Properties: bp elevation ΔT_b = iK_b m, fp depression ΔT_f = iK_f m
  • Henry's Law: solubility of gas ∝ pressure
Practice: How much 12M HCl to make 500 mL of 2M HCl?
V₁ = (2)(0.5)/(12) = 0.083 L = 83 mL

Stoichiometry

  • Mole conversions: mass ↔ moles ↔ particles ↔ volume (gases @STP)
  • Percent Yield: actual/theoretical × 100
  • Limiting Reactant: smallest product amount (after conversion)
  • Empirical/Molecular Formula: lowest ratio (empirical); actual (molecular)
Sample: 10g H₂ + 80g O₂ → H₂O. What's limiting?
O₂ limiting; max H₂O = 80g/32 = 2.5 mol O₂ × 2 = 5 mol H₂O × 18 = 90g H₂O.

Equilibrium & Kinetics

  • Chemical Kinetics: speed of reaction (rate)
  • Collision Theory: particles must collide with right orientation and energy
  • Activation Energy (Ea): energy barrier
  • Catalysts: lower Ea, speed up reaction (not consumed)
  • Equilibrium: rates forward = reverse, concentrations constant
  • Keq = [products]/[reactants]: ignore pure solids/liquids
  • Ksp: solubility product, higher Ksp = more soluble
  • Le Chatelier’s Principle: system responds to minimize stress (concentration, T, P)
Example: What happens to equilibrium if [H₂] increases in N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃?
Shifts right (more NH₃).

Acids, Bases & pH

  • Acids: donate H⁺; Bases: accept H⁺
  • Strong/weak: extent of ionization
  • pH: -log[H⁺]; pOH: -log[OH⁻]
  • Kw: [H⁺][OH⁻]=1×10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C
  • Titration: MaVa = MbVb (for strong acid/base)
Sample: What is pH of 0.01 M HCl?
pH = -log(0.01) = 2
