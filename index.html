Nomenclature & Polyatomic Ions

  • Chemical Nomenclature: The system for naming chemical compounds.
  • Three main classes: Ionic compounds, molecular (covalent) compounds, acids.
  • Correct names reveal what elements/ions are present and how many.
  • Metal + Nonmetal (or polyatomic): Name the cation (positive, metal) first, then the anion (negative, nonmetal or polyatomic).
  • Monatomic anions end in -ide (e.g., Cl- = chloride).
  • For metals with more than one possible charge (usually transition metals), indicate the charge with Roman numerals: FeCl2 is iron(II) chloride.
  • Example: Na2SO4 is sodium sulfate; Cu(NO3)2 is copper(II) nitrate.
  • Nonmetal + Nonmetal: Use prefixes to indicate number of atoms (see table below). Second element ends in -ide.
  • Omit “mono-” on the first element.
  • Example: CO2 = carbon dioxide; N2O5 = dinitrogen pentoxide.

  • Binary Acids: Contain H + nonmetal. Name: hydro- + root + -ic acid.
    Example: HCl (aq) = hydrochloric acid.
  • Oxyacids: Contain H + polyatomic ion (usually containing oxygen).
    - If polyatomic ends in -ate: root + -ic acid.
    - If ends in -ite: root + -ous acid.
    Example: H2SO4 (sulfate → sulfuric acid), H2SO3 (sulfite → sulfurous acid).

Matter & Measurement

  • Matter: Anything that has mass and occupies space. All physical "stuff" in the universe.
  • Types: Substance (element/compound) vs. mixture (homogeneous/heterogeneous).
  • Physical properties: Observed without changing substance identity (color, melting point, density, etc.).
  • Chemical properties: Observed via chemical change (flammability, acidity).
  • States of Matter: Solid, liquid, gas, plasma.

Pure Substance

  • Element: Only one type of atom (O₂, Fe, H₂, etc.)
  • Compound: Two or more elements, chemically combined (H₂O, NaCl, CO₂)

Mixtures

  • Homogeneous (Solution): Uniform throughout (salt water, air)
  • Heterogeneous: Non-uniform, visible phases (sand in water, salad)

When separating mixtures, use physical methods (filtration, distillation, chromatography, evaporation, decanting).

Only compounds can be separated by chemical means (electrolysis, chemical reactions).

  • Physical Change: No new substance (cutting, melting, dissolving, phase changes).
  • Chemical Change: New substance formed (burning, rusting, reacting acids/bases).
Key Signs of Chemical Change:
  • Color change
  • Gas formation (bubbles)
  • Precipitate formation (solid from solution)
  • Heat/light produced or absorbed
Example: Is boiling water a chemical or physical change?
Physical change (only state changes, H₂O molecules stay the same).
Example: Iron rusting?
Chemical change (Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃, new substance formed).
  • Base SI Units:
    • Length: meter (m)
    • Mass: kilogram (kg)
    • Time: second (s)
    • Amount: mole (mol)
    • Temperature: kelvin (K)
  • Prefixes: kilo- (k, 10³), centi- (c, 10⁻²), milli- (m, 10⁻³), micro- (μ, 10⁻⁶), nano- (n, 10⁻⁹)
Example: Convert 500 mg to grams.
500 mg × (1 g / 1000 mg) = 0.5 g
  • Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true value.
  • Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other.
  • Error: Difference between measured and true value.
  • Percent Error: |measured - true| / true × 100
Example: Calculate percent error for a measured mass of 49g (true = 50g)
|49-50|/50 × 100 = 2%

Atoms & The Periodic Table

  • Atom: Smallest unit of matter; consists of protons (+), neutrons (0), electrons (–).
  • Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons, defines the element.
  • Mass Number (A): Protons + neutrons.
  • Isotope: Same element (same Z), different neutrons (different A).
  • Periodic Table: Arranged by atomic number, groups/families (columns), periods (rows).
ParticleSymbolChargeMass (amu)Location
Protonp⁺+11Nucleus
Neutronn⁰01Nucleus
Electrone⁻–1~0Electron cloud
  • Dalton’s Atomic Theory: Atoms are indivisible (now we know they are made of subatomic particles), combine in fixed ratios.
  • Thomson: Discovered electrons (cathode ray tube), “plum pudding” model.
  • Rutherford: Gold foil experiment; atom is mostly empty space, small dense positive nucleus.
  • Bohr: Electrons orbit at set distances (“energy levels”); works well for hydrogen.
  • Quantum Mechanical Model: Electrons in orbitals (probability clouds), not fixed paths.

[Dalton]      [Thomson]        [Rutherford]    [Bohr]             [Modern]
  ⚫             ⚫++--           ⚫ (tiny)         ⚫⎯⎯⦾           ⚫⎯⎯{cloud}
                (e– “in soup”)  (nucleus)    (e– in shells)   (e– in orbitals)
  • Groups (columns): Similar chemical properties, same valence e–
  • Periods (rows): Energy levels (principal quantum number, n)
  • Main families:
    • 1A: Alkali Metals (very reactive, +1)
    • 2A: Alkaline Earth Metals (+2)
    • 7A: Halogens (–1)
    • 8A: Noble Gases (inert, full outer shell)
  • Transition Metals: Variable charges, form colored compounds
  • Metals, Nonmetals, Metalloids: See stair-step on PT for metalloids
Tip: How to find element properties from its location
Group number → valence electrons (for A groups); period number → number of shells.
  • Atomic Radius: Increases ↓ group (more shells); decreases → period (stronger nucleus pulls e– in)
  • Ionization Energy: Decreases ↓ group (e– farther from nucleus, easier to remove); increases → period (more tightly held)
  • Electronegativity: Increases →, decreases ↓ (Fluorine = highest, Francium = lowest)
  • Electron Affinity: More negative →, less negative ↓ (except noble gases)
  • Ionic size: Cations smaller, anions larger than parent atoms
Mnemonic: "F is the greediest!"
Fluorine has the highest electronegativity and electron affinity.
Example: Which atom is larger, Na or K?
K is larger (more energy levels).
  • Isotopes: Same Z, different N (neutrons); identical chemical properties, different mass
  • Average Atomic Mass: Weighted average based on abundance
  • Formula: (mass₁ × %₁ + mass₂ × %₂ + ...) / 100
Practice: Calculate atomic mass for 75% Cl-35, 25% Cl-37
(0.75×35) + (0.25×37) = 26.25 + 9.25 = 35.5 amu
  • Order of fill: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, ...
  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons occupy lowest energy orbitals first
  • Pauli Exclusion: Max 2 e– per orbital (opposite spins)
  • Hund’s Rule: Fill orbitals singly before pairing
  • Noble Gas Notation: Use nearest noble gas in [brackets] as shortcut
Example: Electron configuration of Sulfur (S, Z=16)
1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴ or [Ne] 3s² 3p⁴
  • Misconception: Atomic mass is always a whole number.
    Correction: Atomic mass is often a decimal because it's a weighted average of isotopes.
  • Tip: Memorize 1-2 properties for each major group (alkali, alkaline earth, halogens, noble gases).
  • Misconception: Electrons “orbit” like planets.
    Correction: Electrons are found in orbitals, not fixed paths.

Bonding & Structure

  • Chemical Bond: The attractive force holding atoms or ions together in compounds.
  • Main Types: Ionic (transfer), Covalent (sharing), Metallic (“sea of electrons”).
  • Lewis Dot Structures: Diagrams showing valence electrons and bonding.
  • VSEPR Theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — predicts molecular shapes.
Bond TypeHow FormedPropertiesExample
Ionic Transfer of electrons (metal + nonmetal) High mp/bp, conducts when molten/aq, crystalline NaCl, MgO
Covalent Sharing of electrons (nonmetal + nonmetal) Low mp/bp, not conductive, discrete molecules H2O, CO2
Metallic “Sea of electrons” around positive ions (metal + metal) Conducts, malleable, ductile Fe, Cu, Al
  • Ions: Atoms/molecules with a net charge (cation = +, anion = –).
  • Ionic Compounds: Usually crystalline solids, formula unit shows lowest ratio.
  • Properties:
    • High melting/boiling points
    • Conduct electricity when molten or in solution
    • Brittle, not malleable
  • Electrostatic Forces: Strong attractions between oppositely charged ions.
Example: Why is NaCl solid at room temperature?
Strong ionic bonds require lots of energy to break.

  • Molecular Compounds: Formed from nonmetals sharing electrons.
  • Bond Types:
    • Single: 1 pair shared (H–H)
    • Double: 2 pairs shared (O=O)
    • Triple: 3 pairs shared (N≡N)
  • Lone Pairs: Pairs of electrons not involved in bonding, affect shape (VSEPR).
  • Properties:
    • Low melting/boiling points
    • Poor conductors
    • May exist as gas, liquid, or solid

Q: Why does CO2 have a linear shape?

  • Metallic Bonds: Metal cations surrounded by a “sea” of delocalized electrons.
  • Properties:
    • Conduct electricity & heat
    • Malleable (bendable), ductile (drawn into wire)
    • Shiny (luster)
  • Alloys: Mixtures of metals (brass = Cu + Zn, steel = Fe + C)
Q: Why are metals good conductors?
Free electrons can move and carry current/energy.
  • Electronegativity: Ability of atom to attract e– in a bond (F = highest, Fr = lowest)
  • Bond Polarity:
    • Nonpolar covalent: ∆EN 0–0.2
    • Polar covalent: ∆EN 0.3–1.7
    • Ionic: ∆EN > 1.7
  • Molecular Polarity: Determined by shape and bond polarity (symmetry = nonpolar, asymmetry = polar)
  • Intermolecular Forces:
    • Dispersion (all molecules)
    • Dipole-dipole (polar molecules)
    • Hydrogen bonding (H with N, O, F)

Gases & Gas Laws

  • Gas: A state of matter with indefinite shape and volume, compressible, fills container.
  • Kinetic Molecular Theory: Gases made of particles in constant, random motion; mostly empty space; elastic collisions.
  • Gas Laws: Relate pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and amount (n, moles) of a gas.
  • STP: Standard Temperature & Pressure (0°C, 273.15 K, 1 atm, 22.4 L/mol).
PropertyDefinitionCommon Units
Pressure (P) Force per unit area atm, kPa, mmHg, torr, psi
Volume (V) Space occupied by gas L, mL, cm³
Temperature (T) Average kinetic energy of particles K (Kelvin, always!), °C (for conversion)
Amount (n) Quantity of gas moles (mol)

Conversions: 1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg = 14.7 psi

  • Gases consist of small particles separated by lots of empty space.
  • Collisions are perfectly elastic (no energy loss).
  • Particles are in constant, straight-line motion.
  • Average kinetic energy ∝ Kelvin temperature (KE ∝ T).
  • Explains compressibility, expansion, low density, and diffusion.
Extension: Real vs Ideal Gases
  • Ideal gas: Obeys KMT at all T & P (doesn't exist, but a good model).
  • Real gas: Deviates at low T/high P (particles have volume, IMFs matter).
  • Boyle’s Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (T, n constant) — P↑ V↓ (inverse)
  • Charles’ Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (P, n constant) — T↑ V↑ (direct, T in K!)
  • Gay-Lussac’s Law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (V, n constant) — T↑ P↑
  • Combined Gas Law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ (n constant)
  • Avogadro’s Law: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂ (P, T constant) — more moles, more volume
  • Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT (R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K or 8.31 kPa·L/mol·K)
  • Dalton’s Law: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + ... (sum of partial pressures)
  • Graham’s Law: Rate₁/Rate₂ = √(M₂/M₁) (diffusion/effusion rates)
Mnemonic: “Can Good People Always Party Down?”
Charles, Gay-Lussac, Boyle, Avogadro, Dalton (Gas Laws)
  1. Boyle’s Law Example: A 2.0 L gas at 1.0 atm is compressed to 0.5 L. What is the new pressure?
    Solution
    P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ ⇒ (1.0 atm)(2.0 L) = P₂(0.5 L) ⇒ P₂ = 4.0 atm
  2. Charles’ Law Example: If 1.5 L of gas at 300 K is heated to 450 K at constant pressure, what’s the new volume?
    Solution
    V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ ⇒ 1.5/300 = V₂/450 ⇒ V₂ = 2.25 L
  3. Ideal Gas Law Example: What volume does 3.0 mol O2 occupy at 2 atm, 273 K?
    Solution
    PV = nRT ⇒ V = nRT/P ⇒ V = (3.0)(0.0821)(273)/2 = 33.6 L
  4. Dalton’s Law Example: A mixture has O2 at 0.2 atm, N2 at 0.7 atm, and CO2 at 0.1 atm. What’s the total pressure?
    Solution
    P_total = 0.2 + 0.7 + 0.1 = 1.0 atm
  5. Graham’s Law Example: Which diffuses faster, H2 or O2?
    Solution
    Rate ratio = √(M_O2/M_H2) = √(32/2) = 4. H2 diffuses 4x faster!
  • Always use Kelvin for temperature in gas calculations.
  • Convert volumes to L, pressures to atm or kPa before plugging into equations.
  • Measure gas volume over water? Correct for water vapor pressure (P_gas = P_total - P_water).
Practice: Water vapor correction
If collected gas at 730 mmHg, and water vapor pressure is 30 mmHg, actual gas pressure = 700 mmHg.
  • van der Waals Equation: Corrects for real gas behavior ((P + a(n/V)²)(V-nb) = nRT)
  • High P/low T: Real gases deviate from ideal (IMFs, particle volume).
  • Derivation of root mean square speed: u_rms = √(3RT/M) (M = molar mass in kg/mol)
AP Example: What causes deviation from ideal gas behavior?
IMFs (attraction between molecules) and non-negligible particle volume.
Challenge: Calculate the density of CO2 at STP
D = PM/RT. P=1 atm, M=44 g/mol, R=0.0821, T=273 K ⇒ D = (1*44)/(0.0821*273) ≈ 1.96 g/L
  • Misconception: Temperature in gas laws can be Celsius.
    Correction: Always use Kelvin (K = °C + 273).
  • Tip: For gases at the same T and P, equal volumes mean equal moles (Avogadro’s Law).
  • Misconception: Gases are ideal at all conditions.
    Correction: Only at high T, low P!
  1. A 5.0 L flask contains 0.2 mol of N2 at 27°C. What is the pressure?
    Hint

    Answer
    T = 300 K; P = (0.2 × 0.0821 × 300) / 5.0 = 0.984 atm
  2. If 3.00 L of a gas at 2.00 atm is expanded to 8.00 L at constant temperature, what’s the new pressure?
    Hint

    Answer

    Hint
    Use Graham’s Law
    Answer
    CH₄ effuses about √(146/16) ≈ 3x faster than SF₆.

  • Pressure: Force per unit area.
  • Volume: Space gas occupies.
  • Temperature: Average kinetic energy, must be in K.
  • Mole: Amount of substance, 6.022 × 10²³ particles.
  • Partial pressure: Pressure due to one gas in mixture.
  • Diffusion: Spreading of gas throughout a space.
  • Effusion: Movement through a small hole.

Solutions & Molarity

  • Solution: Homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.
  • Solvent: The substance that does the dissolving (major component; water in aqueous solutions).
  • Solute: The substance being dissolved (minor component; salt in saltwater).
  • Types of Mixtures: Homogeneous (uniform, solutions) vs. Heterogeneous (not uniform, e.g. sand and water).
TermDefinitionExample
SolventDoes the dissolvingWater
SoluteGets dissolvedNaCl
SaturatedMaximum solute dissolvedSweet tea with sugar at limit
UnsaturatedLess than maximum soluteTea with less sugar
SupersaturatedMore than maximum (unstable)Rock candy solution
ElectrolyteConducts when dissolvedNaCl(aq)
NonelectrolyteDoes not conductGlucose(aq)
  • “Like dissolves like”: Polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.
  • Temperature: ↑T usually ↑solubility for solids, ↓solubility for gases.
  • Pressure (gases): ↑P ↑solubility (Henry’s Law: C = kP)
  • Stirring & surface area: ↑speed of dissolving (but not the amount at equilibrium)
Example: Why does CO₂ stay dissolved in cold soda?
Gas solubility is higher at low temperature and high pressure.
  • Molarity (M): M = mol solute / L solution
  • Molality (m): m = mol solute / kg solvent (rare for aqueous chem)
  • Percent by Mass: mass solute / mass solution × 100
  • Percent by Volume: volume solute / volume solution × 100
  • Mole Fraction (X): X = mol component / total mol
  • Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
Example: How many moles in 250 mL of 2.0 M NaCl?
n = M × V = 2.0 mol/L × 0.250 L = 0.50 mol
AP: Why is molality used for boiling point elevation?
Molality is temperature-independent (kg of solvent doesn't change with T, but solution volume can).

    Solution

    • mass = 0.1 × 58.44 = 5.84 g

Stoichiometry & Chemical Calculations

  • Stoichiometry: The quantitative study of reactants and products in a chemical reaction using mole ratios from balanced equations.
  • Main Goal: Predict how much product will form or how much reactant is needed.
  • Always start with a balanced chemical equation!
  • Core skills: Mole conversions, molar mass, Avogadro’s number, percent yield, limiting reactant, solutions and gas law stoichiometry.
            Mass (g)
             /    \
      Molar Mass   \
           |        \
         Moles  <--- Avogadro's Number ---> Particles (atoms/molecules)
           |
        Balanced Equation (Mole Ratios)
           |
         Moles of Product/Reactant
           |
      Molar Mass or Volume (for gases)
           |
       Mass (g), Volume (L at STP), or # Particles
  • **All stoichiometry “paths” go through moles!**
  • Mole: 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro’s number)
  • Molar Mass (MM): g/mol, sum of atomic masses
  • Reactant: Starting material
  • Product: Substance formed
  • Limiting Reactant: Reactant used up first, limits product amount
  • Excess Reactant: Not completely used up
  • Theoretical Yield: Max product predicted by stoichiometry
  • Actual Yield: Amount actually obtained in lab
  • Percent Yield: (Actual/Theoretical) × 100%
  • Empirical Formula: Smallest whole-number ratio
  • Molecular Formula: Actual numbers of each atom
  • **Never start calculations without a balanced equation!**
  • Balance: Atoms on left = atoms on right.
  • Example: __C₂H₆ + __O₂ → __CO₂ + __H₂O
  • Solution: 2C, 6H, 7O → Balanced: 2C₂H₆ + 7O₂ → 4CO₂ + 6H₂O
Practice: Balance Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃
4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃
  • **Core of all stoichiometry:** Use coefficients in balanced equation as mole ratios.
  • Example: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ If you start with 4 mol N₂, how many mol NH₃?
  • Solution: 4 mol N₂ × (2 mol NH₃ / 1 mol N₂) = 8 mol NH₃
ELI5 (Explain Like I’m 5) Version:
Recipe: If you need 1 egg to make 2 pancakes, how many pancakes from 4 eggs? 4 × (2/1) = 8 pancakes!

Mass-Mass Stoichiometry

  1. Convert grams reactant → moles (divide by MM)
  2. Use mole ratio (from balanced eq)
  3. Convert moles product → grams (multiply by MM)
Example:
  • Q: How many grams of CO₂ from 10 g C (MM = 12.0 g/mol) in C + O₂ → CO₂?
  • 10 g C × (1 mol/12.0 g) × (1 mol CO₂/1 mol C) × (44.0 g CO₂/1 mol) = 36.7 g CO₂

Mass-Volume Stoichiometry (Gases @ STP)

  • 1 mol gas @ STP = 22.4 L
  • Example: What volume of O₂ at STP from 16 g S (MM = 32.1) in 2S + 3O₂ → 2SO₃?
  • 16 g S × (1 mol/32.1) × (3 mol O₂/2 mol S) × (22.4 L/1 mol) = 16.8 L O₂

Mass-Particle Stoichiometry

  • Use Avogadro’s Number: 6.022 × 10²³ particles/mol
  • Example: How many molecules of H₂O from 9.0 g H₂O?
  • 9.0 g × (1 mol/18.0 g) × (6.022 × 10²³ molecules/1 mol) = 3.0 × 10²³ molecules
  • Limiting Reactant (LR): Reactant used up first, limits product.
  • How to find LR:
    1. Convert each reactant to moles of product.
    2. Smallest result = LR.
  • Example: 10 g H₂ and 80 g O₂ for 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O. Which is limiting?
  • 10 g H₂ × (1 mol/2.0) × (2 mol H₂O/2 mol H₂) = 5 mol H₂O
    80 g O₂ × (1 mol/32.0) × (2 mol H₂O/1 mol O₂) = 5 mol H₂O
  • If tie, both run out together (rare).
  • Usually, smallest value = LR; other is excess.
Practice: Find excess reactant left over
10 g H₂ forms 5 mol H₂O. To react fully, H₂ uses:
5 mol H₂O × (1 mol O₂/2 mol H₂O) × 32 g/mol = 80 g O₂ needed; if you start with 90 g O₂, 10 g O₂ left over.
  • Theoretical Yield: Calculated (max possible) from stoichiometry.
  • Actual Yield: Lab measurement (often less, due to loss, side rxn, etc).
  • Percent Yield: actual / theoretical × 100
  • Example: Theoretical = 15 g; actual = 12 g; Percent Yield = (12/15) × 100 = 80%
Why less than 100% yield?
Side reactions, incomplete reaction, loss in purification or transfer.
Practice: If you produce 20.0 g of NaCl, but calculated 24.0 g, what is percent yield?

  • For reactions in solution, use molarity to find moles: mol = M × L
  • Example: What volume of 0.500 M HCl is needed to react with 2.00 g NaOH? HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

    1. Convert % to grams (if not already)

    3. Divide all by smallest number of moles
    4. Multiply to clear fractions, if needed

Chemical Equilibrium & Kinetics

  • Chemical Kinetics: Study of the rates (speed) of chemical reactions.
  • Chemical Equilibrium: State where forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate—concentrations stay constant (but not necessarily equal!).
  • Most reactions are reversible to some extent; equilibrium is dynamic, not static.
  • Understanding both is essential for predicting yields, optimizing conditions, and controlling reactions in real life and industry.
  Reaction Progress
   |
   +-----> Kinetics: How fast does it go?
   |
   +-----> Equilibrium: What do you end up with?
              |
    <-------------------------------------------->
    [Reactants] ⇌ [Products]
        ^             |
        |             v
      Forward     Reverse
      Rate        Rate
  • At equilibrium: Ratefwd = Raterev (but not [Reactant] = [Product]!)
  • Rate: Change in concentration per unit time
  • Collision Theory: Particles must collide with correct orientation & energy
  • Activation Energy (Ea): Minimum energy needed to start a reaction
  • Transition State / Activated Complex: High-energy, unstable arrangement during a reaction
  • Catalyst: Substance that lowers activation energy; increases rate; not consumed
  • Equilibrium: State where forward and reverse reactions are equal in rate
  • Equilibrium Constant (K, Keq): Ratio of [products] to [reactants] at equilibrium
  • Le Chatelier's Principle: System at equilibrium shifts to relieve stress (change in concentration, pressure, temperature, volume)
  • Ksp: Solubility product constant (for sparingly soluble salts)
  • Kp: Equilibrium constant using partial pressures
  • Q (Reaction Quotient): Same form as K, but for non-equilibrium conditions
  • Reaction Rate: Δ[Product]/Δt or –Δ[Reactant]/Δt
  • Factors Affecting Rate:
    • Nature of reactants (ions react faster than covalents)
    • Concentration: ↑conc = ↑rate
    • Temperature: ↑T = ↑rate (more particles have E > Ea)
    • Surface area: ↑area = ↑rate (esp. for solids)
    • Catalysts: Lower Ea = ↑rate
  • Collision Theory: Particles must collide with enough energy & correct alignment for reaction to occur (effective collision)
  • Rate Law (Intro): For A + B → C, Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
  • Order of Reaction: m, n (found experimentally)
  • Overall order: m+n
Example: How does a catalyst work?
It provides an alternate, lower-energy pathway for reaction (see PE diagram below).
Potential Energy Diagram 
   Energy
     ^
     |          ____
     |         /    \
     |  _____/        \______
     +------------------------> Reaction Progress
             |____|  |____|
             Ea     Ea with catalyst
Q: If you increase temperature, what happens to reaction rate?
Rate increases because more particles have enough energy to overcome Ea.
  • Mechanism: Series of elementary steps making up overall reaction
  • Intermediate: Species produced in one step, consumed in another (doesn’t appear in net equation)
  • Rate-Determining Step: Slowest step in mechanism—controls overall rate
  • Fast steps can't proceed faster than the slowest one (like a traffic jam)
Example: Multi-step mechanism 
Step 1 (slow):  NO₂ + CO → NO + CO₂
Step 2 (fast):  NO + O₂ → NO₂ + O₂
Overall:       NO₂ + CO + O₂ → NO₂ + CO₂ + O₂
(After canceling intermediates: NO)
Q: How do you find the rate law from a mechanism?
The rate law is based on the reactants of the slow (rate-determining) step.
  • Dynamic Equilibrium: Both forward & reverse reactions occur, but concentrations stay constant (not equal!)
  • Reversible Reaction: A ⇌ B
  • Law of Mass Action (Equilibrium Law):
    • For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
    • Keq = ([C]^c × [D]^d) / ([A]^a × [B]^b)
    • No solids or liquids included in K
  • Magnitude of K:
    • K >> 1: Products favored (mostly product at eq.)
    • K << 1: Reactants favored
    • K ≈ 1: Significant amounts of both
Practice: Write K for 2NO(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)
K = [NO₂]² / ([NO]²[O₂])
Q: What happens if K = 0.01?
Reaction favors reactants at equilibrium.
  • Ksp: For dissolving sparingly soluble salts in water.
  • e.g., PbCl₂(s) ⇌ Pb²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq)
  • Ksp = [Pb²⁺][Cl⁻]² (omit solids!)
  • High Ksp = more soluble; low Ksp = less soluble.
Example: Calculate Ksp if [Ag⁺]=1.3×10⁻⁵, [Cl⁻]=1.3×10⁻⁵
Ksp = (1.3×10⁻⁵)² = 1.7×10⁻¹⁰
Q: What is the effect of a common ion on solubility?
It reduces solubility ("common ion effect").
  • If a system at equilibrium is stressed (change in [ ], P, V, or T), the system shifts to minimize the stress.
  • Increase [reactant]: Shifts right (to product)
  • Increase [product]: Shifts left (to reactant)
  • Decrease volume (gases): Shifts to side with fewer moles of gas
  • Change temperature:
    • Exothermic: ↑T shifts left, ↓T shifts right
    • Endothermic: ↑T shifts right, ↓T shifts left
  • Add catalyst: No shift—only equilibrium reached faster.
Example: What happens if you remove NH₃ from N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃?
Shifts right to produce more NH₃.
Q: If you decrease volume in 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃, which way does it shift?
Right (fewer moles gas on product side: 2 vs 3)
  • Q: Plug current concentrations into K expression (even if not at equilibrium).
  • If Q < K: Reaction goes forward (more products form)
  • If Q > K: Reaction goes backward (more reactants form)
  • If Q = K: System is at equilibrium

  • Rate Law (Integrated):
    • First order: ln[A] = –kt + ln[A]0
    • Half-life (first order): t½ = 0.693/k
  • Arrhenius Equation: k = Ae–Ea/RT
  • Haber Process: Industrial NH₃ synthesis, equilibrium under high pressure/medium temp, iron catalyst
  • Hess’s Law: Add ΔH of steps to get overall reaction ΔH (useful in mechanisms)
  • Kp for gases: Use partial pressures instead of concentrations; Kp = Kc(RT)Δn
  • Link between kinetics and equilibrium: Fast forward and slow reverse means large K (more products); both fast, K ≈ 1.

Acids, Bases & pH

  • Acid: Substance that donates H+ (proton donor).
  • Base: Substance that accepts H+ (proton acceptor).
  • pH: Measure of acidity: pH = -log[H+].
  • pOH: Measure of basicity: pOH = -log[OH-].
  • Kw: Ion-product of water at 25°C: [H+][OH-] = 1.0×10-14.
  • Relationship: pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C.
  • Arrhenius Acid: Produces H+ in aqueous solution.
  • Arrhenius Base: Produces OH- in aqueous solution.
  • Brønsted-Lowry Acid: Proton donor.
  • Brønsted-Lowry Base: Proton acceptor.
  • Lewis Acid: Electron-pair acceptor.
  • Lewis Base: Electron-pair donor.
  • Strong Acid: Completely dissociates in water (e.g., HCl, HNO3).
  • Weak Acid: Partially dissociates (e.g., CH3COOH).
  • Strong Base: Completely dissociates (e.g., NaOH, KOH).
  • Weak Base: Partially dissociates (e.g., NH3).
  • Conjugate Acid/Base: Acid/base formed after proton transfer.
  • Polyprotic Acid: Can donate more than one proton (e.g., H2SO4).
  • Buffer: Solution that resists pH change upon addition of small amounts of acid or base.
  • Indicator: Weak acid/base that changes color at a particular pH range.
  • Arrhenius:
    • Acid → H+ in water
    • Base → OH- in water
  • Brønsted-Lowry:
    • Acid → Proton donor
    • Base → Proton acceptor
    • More general: applies in nonaqueous solvents
  • Lewis:
    • Acid → Electron pair acceptor
    • Base → Electron pair donor
    • Most general: includes many non-proton-transfer reactions
Example: BF3 + NH3
BF3 (Lewis acid) accepts electron pair from NH3 (Lewis base).
  • Strong Acids: HCl, HBr, HI, HNO3, HClO4, H2SO4 (first proton).
  • Properties: Complete dissociation; [H+] = initial acid concentration.
  • Strong Bases: Group 1 hydroxides (NaOH, KOH), Ca(OH)2, Ba(OH)2.
  • Properties: Complete dissociation; [OH-] = initial base concentration × stoichiometric factor.
Example: pH of 0.01 M HCl
Strong acid → [H+] = 0.01 → pH = 2.00
  • Weak Acid: HA ⇌ H+ + A-
  • Ka (Acid Dissociation Constant): Ka = [H+][A-] / [HA]
  • Weak Base: B + H2O ⇌ BH+ + OH-
  • Kb (Base Dissociation Constant): Kb = [BH+][OH-] / [B]
  • Relationship: Ka × Kb = Kw (1.0×10-14)

I:    C        0        0
C:   -x       +x       +x
E:    C-x      x        x
Set Ka = x²/(C-x), solve for x (approximate C≫x).
Example: pH of 0.10 M acetic acid (Ka=1.8×10-5)
ICE: x²/(0.10-x)=1.8×10-5 → x≈√(1.8×10-6)=1.34×10-3
[H+]≈1.34×10-3 → pH ≈ 2.87
  • Percent Ionization: % = ([H+]equil / [HA]initial) × 100
  • Decreases as initial concentration increases (weaker fraction dissociated).
Example: % ionization of 0.10 M CH3COOH
% = (1.34×10-3 / 0.10) × 100 = 1.34%
  • Diprotic: H2A ⇌ H+ + HA- (Ka1); HA- ⇌ H+ + A2- (Ka2).
  • Ka1≫Ka2; treat sequentially.
  • Triprotic (H3A) similar (H3A → H2A- → HA2- → A3-).
Example: pH of 0.10 M H2SO4 (Ka1 large, Ka2=1.2×10-2)
First proton complete; [H+]=0.10 (from first step). Second step: HA- calculation:
ICE: [HA-]=0.10, Ka2=1.2×10-2, solve x; total [H+]≈0.10+x.
  • Buffer: Mixture of weak acid & its conjugate base (or vice versa) that resists pH change.
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A-]/[HA])
  • Useful: Easy pH calculation without ICE table.
Example: pH of acetate buffer (0.10 M CH3COOH and 0.10 M CH3COO-)
pH = 4.74 + log(0.10/0.10) = 4.74
Buffer Capacity & Range
Effective within ±1 pH unit of pKa; greatest when [acid]=[base].
  • Titration: Gradual addition of titrant to analyte until equivalence point (stoichiometric point) is reached.
  • Strong Acid–Strong Base: Equivalence pH ≈ 7.
  • Strong Acid–Weak Base: Equivalence pH < 7.
  • Weak Acid–Strong Base: Equivalence pH > 7.
  • Polyprotic Titrations: Multiple equivalence points for each proton.
Indicator Choice
Choose indicator whose pKa ≈ pH at equivalence point.
Example: Titrate 25 mL 0.10 M CH3COOH with 0.10 M NaOH.
Equivalence at 25 mL added; pH > 7. Use phenolphthalein (pKa ~9).
  • Salt of strong acid + strong base: neutral (pH ≈ 7).
  • Salt of strong acid + weak base: acidic.
  • Salt of weak acid + strong base: basic.
  • Salt of weak acid + weak base: pH depends on relative Ka, Kb.
Example: pH of 0.10 M NH4Cl
NH4+ is conjugate acid of weak base NH3: pH = (pKa+ pKw-pKb)/2 approximate.
  • Addition of common ion reduces ionization of weak acid/base or solubility of salt.
  • Used to shift equilibrium (Le Chatelier’s) in acid/base and solubility problems.
Example: Solubility of AgCl in NaCl solution
AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag+ + Cl-; adding Cl- shifts left, reduces solubility.
